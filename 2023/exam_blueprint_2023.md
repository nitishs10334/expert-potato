# Pattern Analysis

- **Favourite question styles:**
  - *Quantitative:* The examiner highly prefers combining 2-3 concepts into a single question. For example, testing efficiency within time & work, or finding the volume of a frustum alongside water flow rate (time/distance).
  - *Verbal:* Emphasizes direct vocabulary knowledge (idioms, spelling, one-word substitutions) and reading comprehension. There is a noticeable absence of heavy grammar transformation (voice/narration).
  - *Reasoning:* A strong inclination towards series (number and figural), coding-decoding, and constraint-based puzzles (seating arrangements, day puzzles).
  - *General Awareness:* Current events are the primary driver, often interlaced with static topics (e.g., asking about an economic event from late 2022).
  - *Computer Applications:* Practical and applied knowledge, specifically evaluating MS Excel formulas and keyboard shortcuts, over purely theoretical history of computers.

- **Frequently combined concepts:**
  - Profit/Loss + Discount (Marked Price mechanics).
  - Time/Distance + Mensuration (Water flow in pipes/tanks).
  - Mixture & Alligation + Ratio & Proportion.

- **Typical distractor patterns:**
  - *Quant:* Providing intermediate calculation steps as options (e.g., the area of the triangle when the question asks for the remaining area of the circle).
  - *Verbal:* Spelling distractors are phonetically similar but visually incorrect.
  - *Reasoning:* In series and coding, distractors follow slight miscalculations of letter shifts (+2 instead of +3).

- **Difficulty distribution:**
  - The paper skews heavily towards **Medium**. There are rarely any extremely easy, one-second answer questions in Quant. General Awareness is strictly binary (you know it or you don't), making specific current event questions function as "Hard" for most candidates.

- **Numerical vs conceptual balance:**
  - High numerical intensity. The Quant section is calculation-heavy. Even the Reasoning section relies heavily on mathematical operations and numerical series rather than pure abstract logic. The Computer section includes evaluating mathematical formulas in Excel.

- **Direct formula vs reasoning based:**
  - Quant is mostly reasoning-based. Straightforward formula application is rare (except for Simple Interest). Most questions require deducing the relationship before plugging numbers into a formula.

# Recommendations

If generating 10 guess papers, the distribution should NOT simply mirror the written syllabus equally. The examiner clearly has biases. The structure of the mock papers should be weighted as follows:

1. **Quantitative Ability (16 Questions):**
   - **Do not include** Trigonometry, Data Interpretation, or advanced 3D shapes (Pyramids, Prisms). These take up space and were completely ignored by the examiner.
   - **Heavily represent (10-12 questions):** Arithmetic word problems (Profit/Loss, Time/Work, Speed/Distance, Percentages, Mixtures). Ensure these are multi-step problems, not direct formula plugs.
   - **Moderately represent (4-6 questions):** Basic algebra (linear equations), number properties (divisibility), and fundamental mensuration (circles, cones, triangles).

2. **Verbal Ability (16 Questions):**
   - **Do not include** Active/Passive voice or Direct/Indirect speech conversions.
   - **Heavily represent:** Reading Comprehension (1 passage with 3 questions is mandatory), Idioms (3 questions), Spellings (2 questions), and Paragraph Jumbles (2 questions).
   - **Moderately represent:** One-word substitution (2 questions), Sentence improvement (2 questions), and Synonyms/Antonyms (2 questions). 

3. **Logical & Numerical Reasoning (22 Questions):**
   - **Do not include** vague logic topics like "Emotional Intelligence", "Social Intelligence", or complex folding/punching puzzles.
   - **Heavily represent (12-14 questions):** Sequence and series (Number, Letter, Figure series), Coding-Decoding (especially number-based), and Seating Arrangements (Linear and Circular).
   - **Moderately represent (8-10 questions):** Blood relations (include coded blood relations), BODMAS mathematical operations, Syllogisms, and simple visual reasoning (Dice/Mirrors).

4. **General Awareness (16 Questions):**
   - Frame 60-70% of the section around **Current Events** (last 6-12 months). Even for static topics like Economics or Science, find a recent news trigger to base the question on.
   - Dedicate 3-4 questions to History and Culture (focus on specific timelines or niche cultural facts).
   - Include 1 question each on Geography, Constitution, and Books/Authors.

5. **Knowledge in Computer Applications (10 Questions):**
   - Skip deep dives into E-Governance or the IT Act.
   - Ensure at least 2-3 questions require the student to evaluate a specific MS Excel formula or know a specific MS Word/Windows shortcut.
   - Devote the rest to classifying hardware (RAM vs ROM) and software (System vs Utility). The questions should be practical and definition-based.
